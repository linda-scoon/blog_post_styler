# How the experts map a place — and how izebuy will map a market

A synthesis of (a) how Google builds a map, (b) the science of how humans navigate,
and (c) how informal places get mapped in the developing world — turned into a
repeatable field procedure for one writer, on foot, with only a phone, pen and paper.

---

## Part 1 — The three playbooks the experts use

### A. Kevin Lynch — the structure of any navigable place *(the theory)*
In *The Image of the City* (1960), Lynch showed that people build a mental map of any
place out of **five element types**. They map almost perfectly onto a market interior:

| Lynch element | In a market |
| --- | --- |
| **Paths** — the routes you move along | the lanes / aisles between stalls |
| **Edges** — boundaries | the market's outer limit, the stream, the main road, a fence |
| **Districts** — areas with one character | the trade zones: clothes, produce, fish, food |
| **Nodes** — focal points where paths meet / people gather | the main gate, lane junctions, the busiest crossing |
| **Landmarks** — fixed external reference points | the mill, a painted shop, a big tree, the church |

A place is **"legible"** when these five are clear. Making a market legible *on the
page* is exactly our writer's job.
*(Source: Nasar / Lynch legibility research — ScienceDirect S0272494412000096, peer-reviewed.)*

### B. Google "Ground Truth" — the production process *(the workflow)*
Google doesn't map everything at once; it builds in **layers, in a fixed order**:

1. **Outline first** — start from an authoritative base footprint (the shape of the place).
2. **Traverse & capture** — drive/walk it and photograph; Google now even extracts
   business names and details from Street View imagery automatically.
   *(Source: research.google blog, "Updating Google Maps with Deep Learning and Street View" — primary.)*
3. **Add POIs with attributes** — points of interest get a **category and attributes**
   (name, type, opening hours) — not just dots on a map.
4. **Verify on the ground** — check against the real world and authoritative sources;
   corrections come from local people (Local Guides).
5. **Render legibly** — keep the finished map **uncluttered**: show what matters, hide
   the rest, so it stays readable.

**The repeatable algorithm:** outline → traverse & capture → classify into zones/POIs
with attributes → verify on the ground → present legibly. We follow the same order, at
the scale of one market.

### C. The wayfinding science — *why landmarks beat left/right*
The cognitive-science literature is consistent and was the one claim the research
harness fully verified:

- **Landmarks placed at decision points (junctions) are the single most effective
  navigation aid**, and routes described by landmarks are recalled and followed better
  than turn-by-turn or compass directions.
  *(Sources: Springer Cognitive Processing 2021 review s10339-021-01012-x; Nature npj Science of Learning 2022; multiple peer-reviewed — verified, none refuted.)*
- **A good landmark passes three tests: permanent, visible from several approaches,
  and unique** (so it isn't confused with its neighbours).
  *(Source: MIT wayfinding design principles.)*
- This is already how people navigate addressless African towns — *"behind the mill,"
  "by the big tree," "past the church"* — so it's natural for both our writers and our
  readers. *(Sources: "Walk Straight" study, South Africa; AFP "Streets with no names".)*

**The rule that falls out of this:** anchor everything to **structure, not stock.** A
stall's goods change daily; the mill, the tree and the painted shop do not. Map the
permanent things.

### D. It has been done before, informally, by people like ours
- **Map Kibera** (Nairobi) — residents mapped an entire informal settlement *including
  its markets* on foot, recording **attributes** (what's sold, hours, services), not
  just locations. The closest real precedent to our project. *(mapkibera.org; OSM wiki.)*
- **Stall-by-stall market enumeration** is a published, standard method — researchers
  have walked East-African markets recording every stall by zone.
  *(NIH/PMC market-sampling study; Habitat International S0264275122000476.)*
- **The low-tech field kit is proven:** sketch-map by hand, **pace** distances
  (calibrate your stride over 100 m, ~3–4% error), **time** each walk, photograph as
  you go, digitise afterward — the Humanitarian OpenStreetMap "Field Papers" loop and
  HeiGIT Sketch Map Tool exist precisely for **no-device, single-person** mapping.
  *(learnosm.org; HOT field-survey methodology — primary; heigit.org Sketch Map Tool.)*

## Part 2 — The izebuy market-mapping field procedure
**One writer, on foot, phone + pen + paper. No survey kit, no 3D camera, no car.**
Built for an informal market: dust paths, natural and painted landmarks, fixed stalls.

> The order matters — it's the experts' order (outline → capture → classify → verify →
> present), shrunk to one market.

**Step 0 — Arrive and take the cover shot.** Photograph the market as you walk in from
the main entrance. This is the reader's first sight of the place. Name it after the
market (`mbayani-market.jpg`).

**Step 1 — Walk the edge first.** Walk the market's outer boundary once to learn its
shape and find every entrance/gate. Note the natural edges — a stream, the main road, a
fence, a hill. *(Lynch's edges; Google's "outline first".)*

**Step 2 — Mark the nodes.** The main gate and the junctions where lanes meet and
crowds flow. These are where a reader makes decisions, so they're where directions and
landmarks must live.

**Step 3 — Name the districts by trade.** One slow pass through the lanes, labelling the
zones by what's sold — clothes, produce, fish, food, hardware, butchery. Markets cluster
by trade, so these zones are stable even though the sellers change. *(Lynch's districts.)*

**Step 4 — Choose your landmarks — the permanent ones only.** For each node/zone, pick
**one** strong reference point and test it: *Is it permanent? Visible from a distance and
several directions? Unique?* Use natural anchors (a big tree, the stream, a hill) and
durable built ones (the maize mill, a painted or named shop, the church, a borehole).
**Never** anchor to a trader or their goods. Keep landmarks sparse — one good one per
decision point beats five. *(Wayfinding triad: permanent / visible / unique.)*

**Step 5 — Trace the paths between districts, landmark to landmark.** Work out the route
a newcomer walks to get from one zone to the next, described only by things they can see:
*"from the gate, past the green shop, down the slope to the produce."* No left/right, no
compass.

**Step 6 — Pace and time every leg.** Calibrate your stride over a known distance before
you start. Then pace lane lengths and **time each walk on your phone's stopwatch.** Write
the *real* numbers — "about a two-minute walk," because you measured it, not guessed.

**Step 7 — Photograph systematically.** One shot per **zone** (named after it), one per
**landmark**, and representative **stalls/shops**. Name every file after what it shows —
the filename is how the image lands in the right place on the page.

**Step 8 — Draw the map.** By hand: the boundary, the gates, the main paths, the district
blocks, and the labelled landmarks. Useful beats neat. Photograph it (`mbayani-map.jpg`).
This is the centrepiece of the article.

**Step 9 — Write the guided walk.** First person, entrance → zone → zone, navigating
landmark to landmark, with the recorded times and a **golden nugget** per zone.

**Step 10 — The legibility check (verify, like Ground Truth does).** Re-read it as a
stranger: *Could I find the fish from the gate? Is every landmark permanent? Are the
times real? Is anything cluttered or vague?* Fix, then submit.
